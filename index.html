Group Work · Session 1

Case Description

For this course, our group invented a design case around the question: how can we check on the wellbeing of students? After exploring multiple target groups — students aged 18–24, children aged 5–12, and the elderly — we chose to focus on university students (18–24) and their overall wellbeing.

The platform we're designing for is the MiRo robot, a biomimetic animal-inspired robot developed by Consequential Robotics. MiRo can communicate verbally through a built-in speaker and express itself physically through an actuated head, ears, tail, eyes, and locomotion system.

Our scenarios range from general wellbeing check-ins using a curated set of wellbeing questions, to guided breathing exercises, study session support, and nudging students to reconnect with friends or family. Each scenario is designed to feel natural, warm, and non-intrusive for a student context.

The wellbeing questions we drew on cover dimensions like hydration, sleep, physical activity, social connection, emotional state, and sense of effectiveness — prompting small, actionable self-care steps rather than overwhelming the student.

Research Question

"How can the MiRo robot be expanded upon to help students (ages 18–24) with their overall well-being?"

Target Group

University students aged 18–24. A group facing unique pressures — academic load, social adjustment, irregular routines — and limited access to consistent wellbeing support.

Design Tools

We selected three complementary methods from the User Innovation Toolkit that together let us imagine, visualise, and evaluate how MiRo could interact with students.

🔭
Future-oriented
Scenario Analysis

A process that enables you to envision a diversity of futures by distinguishing likely from less likely transitions and identifying the key determinants of each scenario.

For our case, scenario analysis lets us walk through concrete futures — a student sits down at their desk, MiRo initiates a check-in, questions are asked and answered. We can inhabit the student's perspective and reason through each moment. We also use it to analyse the situation before MiRo is introduced, making visible what problems actually exist in the student's daily routine.

🎬
Narrative & Visual
Storyboard

A sequence of images that visualises how a persona uses a product or service — grounding abstract ideas in concrete, observable moments.

A storyboard brings MiRo to life: a panel showing MiRo's tail wagging as a student answers a question, or the robot guiding a breathing exercise with timed head movements. Where scenario analysis plays through the logic, storyboarding captures the emotion — and communicates our design intent to stakeholders far more vividly than words alone.

🗺️
User Experience
Experience Map

A visualisation of how a user moves through a product or service over time, mapping emotional highs and lows across different touchpoints.

Our most revealing application: creating two experience maps of the same scenario — one without MiRo, one with — and placing them side by side. This comparison shows exactly where MiRo adds emotional value and which moments in the student's day deserve the most thoughtful design attention.

System Components

Building Blocks

MiRo's design is broken into three layers — what the robot does physically, how it behaves in software, and how it looks and feels to the student.

⚙️
Hardware
  • Built-in actuated eyes and 3 DoF neck — sufficient for expressive physical communication
  • Additional rear speaker attachment for clearer audio during questions
  • Touch-activated heart rate / physiological sensors for tailored responses
  • Integrated timer module for structuring breathing exercises
  • Expanded actuators for a "dance mode" to lift student mood
💻
Software
  • Built-in speaker enables MiRo to ask questions and guide scenarios verbally
  • Wizard-of-Oz approach: a human operator can puppeteer speech and physical expressions in real time
  • In principle, all behaviour can be Wizard-of-Oz'd — lowering technical barriers for early-stage testing
  • Scripted dialogue trees for repeatable wellbeing check-in flows
🎨
Outer Design
  • Custom accessories — different ears, collars, or clothes — to suit the scenario
  • Study-themed add-ons (e.g. a tiny graduation cap) to contextualise MiRo for university students
  • Playful extras (wings, themed costumes) to make the robot feel approachable, not clinical
  • Design philosophy: warm and expressive, never sterile or intimidating

System Structure

Functional Breakdown

MiRo's functions divide into two core channels — verbal and physical — both serving the overarching goal of detecting and responding to student wellbeing. These feed into a set of concrete scenarios.

🗣️ Verbal Communication
Asking questions Replying to student Guided exercises

MiRo uses its speaker to initiate check-ins, ask targeted wellbeing questions (covering hydration, sleep, activity, mood, and social connection), and guide students through structured scenarios like breathing exercises or study breaks. Dialogue can be Wizard-of-Oz'd or scripted.

🐾 Physical Expressions
Head nodding Tail wagging Ear turning Eye blinking Locomotion

MiRo's body language reinforces its verbal channel: nodding to acknowledge a student's response, wagging its tail to express warmth, turning its ears to signal attention, and moving towards the student to create physical presence and a sense of care.

🧩 Scenarios
Overall wellbeing Breathing exercise Study session Call a friend

Each scenario defines a specific interaction mode: a general check-in using the wellbeing question list, a structured breathing exercise with timed guidance, a study session break reminder, or a social nudge encouraging the student to call a friend or family member.

🔍 Detecting Wellbeing
Self check-in Health monitoring Checking in on others

Wellbeing is detected primarily through conversation — the student self-reports. Optional physiological sensors (heart rate via touch) could add an objective layer. Over time, pattern tracking across check-ins would allow MiRo to surface trends rather than just respond to individual moments.

Potential Experimental Approach

Experimental Design

We propose using theatre exercises — low-tech, human-enacted simulations — to rapidly test how students respond to MiRo interactions before committing to hardware or software development. A human plays the role of MiRo; students respond naturally.

01

Response to Check-In

A human plays MiRo initiating a basic wellbeing check-in. We observe: do students engage? Feel comfortable? Dismiss it? This establishes a baseline for whether the interaction feels welcome in a student context.

02

Types of Check-In

Comparing different interaction styles — proactive vs. reactive, gentle vs. direct, routine vs. spontaneous — each enacted and observed. The goal is to find what tone and timing feels most natural and trustworthy for students.

03

Question Themes

Testing different wellbeing question categories (hydration, sleep, social connection, physical activity, mood) to find which resonate most with students, and which feel irrelevant or too intrusive for a robot to ask.

04

Prompt Responses & Facial Expression

Recording both verbal answers and facial expressions in response to specific prompts. This gives qualitative emotional data that supplements self-reported responses — capturing reactions students may not consciously articulate.

Session 2: Story Building

Overview

Session 2 explores the role of storytelling and narrative design in human-robot interaction (HRI). Through scenario-based design methods, we investigated how stories can help us understand, communicate, and critically evaluate robot behaviors and interactions in care settings.

Why Storytelling Matters in HRI

As emphasized in the lecture, storytelling is fundamental to how humans understand and relate to technology. Stories serve multiple purposes in social robot design:

  • Exploration: Stories help us imagine and test possible futures before building expensive prototypes
  • Communication: Scenarios provide a shared language between designers, stakeholders, and users
  • Critical reflection: Narratives reveal assumptions and potential problems in our designs
  • Expectation management: The stories we tell shape how people perceive and interact with robots

Paepcke & Takayama (2010) demonstrated that setting expectations through narratives significantly influences perceived robot competence—with higher expectations leading to harsher judgments when robots underperform.

Our Design Tool: The HRI Office-Script Template

We developed a hybrid scenario-building tool that combines structured scriptwriting with improvisational documentary-style reflection. This approach was grounded in research on scripting for participatory design and design documentaries.

Design Rationale

Our tool draws inspiration from:

  • Scenario-based design: Provides structure for exploring robot interactions in context (van der Bijl-Brouwer & van der Voort, 2013)
  • Documentary film methods: Allows for capturing multiple perspectives and reflective commentary (Raijmakers et al., 2006)
  • Improvisational theatre: Enables exploration of unexpected interactions and emergent behaviors (Dertien et al., 2024)

The Template Structure

Setup:

[Describe the setup of the scenario, where is it, when is it, who is on screen, any important people for the scenario that are off-screen]

Robot [name]:

  • Action: [Describe the function of the robot. What is it doing?]
  • Intent: [Describe the purpose. Why is it doing that?]
  • Emotion: [Describe the feeling/emotion behind the action and intent.]

[Insert your usergroup]:

  • Action: [Describe the action of the user. What are they doing?]
  • Intent: [Describe the purpose. Why are they doing that?]
  • Emotion: [Describe the feeling/emotion behind the action and intent.]

OFFICE-Breakout: [question asked]

[Include a general funny comment or reflection about the scene. It could be mocking the scenario or interaction. It is set up in an interview documentary style as if the character/robot is reflecting on what had just happened.]

[Repeat as necessary]

Why This Structure Works

The template addresses specific challenges in HRI design:

  • Separates action, intent, and emotion: Forces designers to think beyond surface behaviors to underlying motivations
  • Documentary breakouts: Provides space for reflection and critical commentary, preventing "rosy stories" that over-promise robot capabilities
  • Flexibility: Allows improvisation within structure, capturing unexpected interactions while maintaining focus
  • Multiple perspectives: Explicitly includes both human and robot viewpoints

References: Raijmakers et al. (2006) "Design documentaries: inspiring design research through documentary film"; Dertien et al. (2024) "Improvisation Theatre as HRI simulation tool"

Scenarios: MIRO-E in Study Settings

We applied our tool to explore interactions with a MIRO-E robot designed to support student wellbeing during study sessions. Two scenarios were developed to explore different aspects of the interaction.

Scenario 1: Wellbeing Check-In (Frequent Use)

Context: Active classroom with students working on homework. MIRO robot autonomously monitors study patterns and performs wellbeing interventions.

Setup: Student has been typing for 40 minutes without a break. MIRO approaches to suggest a breathing exercise.

Key Interaction:

  • Robot behavior: Ears perk forward, light brightens, moves closer in 30-second cycles until acknowledged
  • Robot intent: Check in on student wellbeing, offer supportive intervention
  • Student state: Focused, stressed, wearing headphones—initially annoyed but ultimately accepting

Design Tension: This scenario explores the balance between supportive care and intrusive interruption. When does wellbeing monitoring feel helpful versus controlling?

Office-Style Reflection:

MIRO: "Well I'm glad the student did a relaxing exercise. It seemed very necessary!"

Student: "I feel a lot better now. Wheeew"

Insights: The scenario reveals how robot persistence can be perceived differently depending on student state. The documentary format allowed us to capture the internal conflict—annoyance versus recognition of need—that wouldn't be visible in traditional scenario descriptions.

Scenario 2: Deaf Student (Edge Case)

Context: Same classroom setting, but MIRO encounters a deaf student who cannot hear verbal instructions.

Setup: MIRO approaches student and attempts verbal communication, which goes unheard. Robot escalates attempts to get attention.

Key Interaction:

  • Robot behavior: Increases volume, adds movement (back and forth), lights, sounds—growing frustration evident in behavior
  • Robot emotion: Frustration, confusion at lack of response
  • Student response: Eventually looks up, confused by erratic robot behavior with no understanding of intent

Design Failure: This scenario exposes a critical accessibility gap—the robot lacks alternative communication modalities and has no way to recognize or adapt to deafness.

Office-Style Reflection:

Deaf student: "This robot is acting really weird. I have no idea what to think of this."

Insights: Edge cases reveal design assumptions. The robot was designed assuming auditory communication, without fallback strategies. The escalating frustration behavior also raises questions about robot emotion displays—should robots show frustration with users? The scenario makes these issues visceral and memorable rather than abstract.

This reflects warnings from the lecture about technology being "used only and exactly in the way it was intended"—designs must account for diverse users and unexpected contexts.

Evaluation of Our Tool

Strengths

  • Reveals hidden assumptions: The structured format forced us to articulate robot emotions and intents we hadn't consciously considered
  • Balances structure and flexibility: Template provides consistency while allowing improvisation
  • Multiple perspectives: Documentary breakouts capture what characters are thinking, not just what they do
  • Memorable: The Office-style format makes scenarios more engaging and easier to discuss with stakeholders
  • Exposes gaps: Edge case scenario immediately highlighted accessibility issues we hadn't considered

Limitations & Improvements

  • Still requires interpretation: Script doesn't capture full physicality—would benefit from video prototyping or theatre simulation
  • Risk of anthropomorphization: Assigning emotions to robots might reinforce unrealistic expectations
  • Needs iteration: Documentary breakouts could be structured more systematically with specific prompts
  • Limited validation: Should be tested with actual students and care workers to validate scenarios

Grounding in Literature

Our tool aligns with established HRI design methods:

  • Follows scenario-based design principles (van der Bijl-Brouwer & van der Voort, 2013) while adding the documentary layer for critical reflection
  • Addresses the need for HRI-specific tools highlighted in lecture—incorporates robot emotional states and interaction patterns unique to social robotics
  • Builds on research showing improvisation theatre's value for HRI simulation (Dertien et al., 2024)
  • Incorporates lessons from science fiction prototyping (Johnson, 2011) about exploring both utopian and dystopian futures

Key Takeaways

This session reinforced several critical lessons for social robot design:

  1. Stories shape expectations: The narratives we create around robots directly influence how people interact with and judge them. We must be careful not to tell "rosy stories" that set up unrealistic expectations.
  2. Edge cases matter: The deaf student scenario revealed more about our design assumptions than the typical use case. Critical and speculative scenarios are essential for robust design.
  3. Tools need tailoring: Generic scenario methods don't capture HRI-specific concerns like robot emotional expression, expectation management, and the social dynamics of human-robot encounters. We need HRI-specific tools.
  4. Multiple perspectives are essential: Understanding both human and robot "viewpoints" (even if the robot's is designed) helps identify interaction breakdowns and design opportunities.
  5. Storytelling is a design tool, not just communication: Creating scenarios doesn't just help us explain designs—it actively shapes and improves them by forcing us to think through details we'd otherwise miss.
